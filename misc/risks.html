Technology Risks

Digital simulations and serious gaming are an interesting and expanding area of education. They offer potential benefits in terms of student engagement, motivation, and learning outcomes. However, there's a dearth of clear empirical evidence regarding their effectiveness, and thus, the adoption of these tools largely falls on the instructor or institution's discretion. The lack of a comprehensive policy or guidelines for their integration into educational systems is also a challenge. Furthermore, the varied definitions and terminologies surrounding digital simulations and games create some "terminological ambiguity," which further complicates the field

The risks associated with the use of digital simulations and serious games in education include the following:

1. Online Rudeness: The anonymous nature of the digital world can sometimes lead to inappropriate behavior and online rudeness. Students may engage in cyberbullying or exhibit disrespectful behavior towards others within the gaming environment. It is important to address this risk by promoting online etiquette and teaching students how to communicate respectfully and responsibly in virtual settings. Educators can provide clear guidelines on appropriate behavior and create a safe and inclusive online environment where students can learn and collaborate without fear of harassment or mistreatment.

2. Barrier to Physical Play: Digital simulation games are often sedentary activities that require students to spend extended periods of time in front of screens. This can lead to a reduction in physical activity and exercise, which is essential for students' overall health and well-being. To mitigate this risk, educators should encourage a balanced approach by incorporating physical play and exercise into the curriculum. They can incorporate movement breaks or outdoor activities that complement the use of digital simulations and serious gaming, ensuring that students maintain a healthy and active lifestyle.

3. Addictive Nature of Games: Digital games, including simulations and serious gaming, are designed to be engaging and immersive, often employing game mechanics that make them addictive. Students may become excessively focused on gameplay, leading to excessive screen time and potential addiction to these games. To address this risk, it is important to establish clear guidelines for game usage and screen time limits. Educators should encourage responsible gaming habits and educate students about the potential risks of excessive gaming. It is also crucial to provide a balanced curriculum that includes a variety of educational activities beyond digital gaming to ensure a well-rounded learning experience.

4. Curriculum Alignment: Not all games are designed with educational goals in mind. It is essential to ensure that the games used in educational settings align with the curriculum and support students' learning objectives. Games that are not specifically designed for educational purposes may lack the necessary content or instructional elements to effectively contribute to students' learning outcomes. Educators should carefully evaluate and select games that align with the curriculum, ensuring that they enhance the learning experience and facilitate the achievement of educational goals.

5. High Costs of Equipment and Digital Divide: The integration of digital simulations and serious gaming often requires access to specific technological equipment, such as computers, gaming consoles, or virtual reality devices. However, not all students may have equal access to these resources, leading to a digital divide. Students from disadvantaged backgrounds or schools with limited resources may face barriers in accessing and benefiting from digital simulations and serious gaming. To address this risk, educators and institutions should work towards reducing the digital divide by providing equitable access to technology and exploring alternative delivery methods that can accommodate students with limited resources.Moreover, the time investment involved in developing these simulations can be significant, posing challenges for organizations with limited resources or strict deadlines (Hurrell, n.d.). The rapid pace of technological advancements also means that digital simulations need frequent updates and adjustments to remain relevant, resulting in ongoing maintenance expenses (Knilt, n.d.). Additionally, extensive training is necessary for staff members to effectively handle and utilize this technology, adding to the time and financial investment required (Knilt, n.d.). Furthermore, the complexity of digital simulation development necessitates specialized technical expertise in programming, graphic design, and animation, which may not always be readily available (Hurrell, n.d.). There may also be accessibility issues, as some learners may have disabilities or encounter technical difficulties that hinder their effective use of digital simulations (Hurrell, n.d.). Moreover, the transferability of skills learned through digital simulations to real-world situations may be limited, as these simulations may not always accurately replicate real-life scenarios (Hurrell, n.d.). Finally, regardless of the precision of the digital simulation, there is always room for error and uncertainty in recreating real-life scenarios (Knilt, n.d.).

6. Over-Reliance on Technology: While digital simulations and serious gaming can enhance the learning experience, there is a risk of over-reliance on technology. Students may become dependent on digital tools and simulations to the extent that they rely solely on these resources for learning, neglecting other essential learning activities and strategies. To mitigate this risk, educators should encourage a balanced approach to learning that incorporates a variety of instructional methods and resources. They should emphasize the importance of critical thinking, problem-solving, and independent learning skills beyond the scope of digital simulations and serious gaming.
