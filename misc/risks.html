Technology Risks

Digital simulations and serious gaming are expanding areas of education with potential benefits in terms of student engagement, motivation, and learning outcomes. However, the effectiveness of these tools lacks clear empirical evidence, leaving their adoption to the discretion of instructors and institutions. The absence of comprehensive policies and guidelines for their integration further complicates their use. Additionally, the varied definitions and terminologies surrounding digital simulations and games create ambiguity in the field. These challenges call for more research, evidence-based studies, standardized frameworks, and clear terminology to facilitate the effective integration of digital simulations and serious gaming into education.

The risks associated with the use of digital simulations and serious games in education are outlined below:

1) Online Rudeness: The anonymous nature of the digital world can sometimes lead to inappropriate behavior and disrespectful conduct among students, including cyberbullying. To mitigate this risk, it is crucial to promote online etiquette and teach students how to communicate respectfully and responsibly in virtual settings. Educators can play a vital role in addressing this issue by providing clear guidelines on appropriate behavior and fostering a safe and inclusive online environment. By setting expectations and actively monitoring interactions, educators can create a positive atmosphere where students feel comfortable learning and collaborating without the fear of harassment or mistreatment.

2) Barrier to Physical Play: Digital simulation games are often sedentary activities that require students to spend extended periods of time in front of screens. This can lead to a reduction in physical activity and exercise, which is essential for students' overall health and well-being. To mitigate this risk, educators should encourage a balanced approach by incorporating physical play and exercise into the curriculum. They can incorporate movement breaks or outdoor activities that complement the use of digital simulations and serious gaming, ensuring that students maintain a healthy and active lifestyle.

3) Addictive Nature of Games: Digital games, including simulations and serious gaming, are designed to be engaging and immersive, often employing game mechanics that make them addictive. Students may become excessively focused on gameplay, leading to excessive screen time and potential addiction to these games. To address this risk, it is important to establish clear guidelines for game usage and screen time limits. Educators should encourage responsible gaming habits and educate students about the potential risks of excessive gaming. It is also crucial to provide a balanced curriculum that includes a variety of educational activities beyond digital gaming to ensure a well-rounded learning experience.

4) Curriculum Alignment: It is crucial to ensure that the games used in educational settings align with the curriculum and support students' learning objectives. Not all games are designed with educational goals in mind, so careful evaluation and selection are necessary. Games that are not specifically designed for educational purposes may lack the necessary content or instructional elements to effectively contribute to students' learning outcomes. Educators should prioritize games that align with the curriculum, enhancing the learning experience and facilitating the achievement of educational goals. By selecting appropriate games, educators can maximize the educational value and relevance of gaming in the classroom.

5) High Costs of Equipment and Digital Divide: The integration of digital simulations and serious gaming often comes with a requirement for specific technological equipment, such as computers, gaming consoles, or virtual reality devices. However, not all students have equal access to these resources, leading to a digital divide. Students from disadvantaged backgrounds or schools with limited resources may face barriers in accessing and benefiting from digital simulations and serious gaming. To address this risk, educators and institutions should work towards reducing the digital divide by striving to provide equitable access to technology and exploring alternative delivery methods that can accommodate students with limited resources.

Additionally, the development of digital simulations entails significant time investment, which can pose challenges for organizations with limited resources or tight deadlines (Hurrell, n.d.). The rapid pace of technological advancements also necessitates frequent updates and adjustments to digital simulations to ensure their relevance, resulting in ongoing maintenance expenses (Knilt, n.d.). Moreover, extensive training is required for staff members to effectively handle and utilize this technology, further adding to the time and financial investment (Knilt, n.d.).

Furthermore, the complexity of digital simulation development requires specialized technical expertise in programming, graphic design, and animation, which may not always be readily available (Hurrell, n.d.). Accessibility issues may arise as some learners may have disabilities or encounter technical difficulties that hinder their effective use of digital simulations (Hurrell, n.d.). It is also important to consider the transferability of skills learned through digital simulations to real-world situations, as these simulations may not always accurately replicate real-life scenarios (Hurrell, n.d.). Regardless of the precision of the digital simulation, there is always room for error and uncertainty when recreating real-life situations (Knilt, n.d.).

6) Over-Reliance on Technology: Although digital simulations and serious gaming can enhance the learning experience, there is a potential risk of over-reliance on technology. Students may become overly dependent on digital tools and simulations, relying solely on these resources for learning and neglecting other important learning activities and strategies. To mitigate this risk, educators should promote a balanced approach to learning that incorporates a variety of instructional methods and resources. It is important to emphasize the value of critical thinking, problem-solving, and independent learning skills that go beyond the scope of digital simulations and serious gaming. By encouraging a diversified learning approach, educators can foster well-rounded skills and ensure that students are not solely dependent on technology for their educational development.
